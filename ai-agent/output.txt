# what was the watergate scandal
**Subject:** history
**Date/Period:** 1972–1974
**Key Figure:** Richard Nixon (key figure); Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein (journalists who broke the story)
**Work/Event:** Watergate Scandal

## Explanation
The Watergate scandal was a massive political crisis in the United States that lasted from 1972 to 1974. It started on June 17, 1972, when five men were caught breaking into the headquarters of the Democratic National Committee (the DNC — the organization that runs the Democratic Party) at the Watergate office building in Washington, D.C. These burglars were connected to President Richard Nixon's re-election campaign. Their goal was to steal documents and plant listening devices to help Nixon win the election. When the break-in was discovered, Nixon and his top advisors tried to hide the White House's involvement — this is called a 'cover-up.' Nixon secretly told the FBI to back off the investigation and paid hush money to keep the burglars quiet. Eventually, the cover-up fell apart. It came out that Nixon had secretly recorded his own conversations in the Oval Office, and those tapes proved he was directly involved in the cover-up. Facing almost certain removal from office by Congress, Nixon resigned on August 8, 1974, becoming the only U.S. president in history ever to resign. In total, 48 government officials were convicted of crimes connected to the scandal.

## Key Points
- On June 17, 1972, five burglars tied to Nixon's re-election committee were caught breaking into Democratic Party headquarters at the Watergate complex in Washington, D.C. — their goal was to steal information and spy on Nixon's political opponents.
- Nixon actively participated in covering up the White House's role, including secretly ordering the FBI to limit its investigation and arranging payments to keep the burglars silent. Secret tape recordings of his Oval Office conversations — called the 'Nixon tapes' — became the 'smoking gun' that proved his guilt.
- Because the House of Representatives was moving to impeach (formally charge and put on trial) him for obstruction of justice, abuse of power, and contempt of Congress, Nixon resigned on August 8, 1974 — the only U.S. president ever to do so. His successor, President Gerald Ford, pardoned him one month later, meaning Nixon faced no criminal punishment. A total of 48 officials were convicted of crimes related to the scandal.
- Investigative journalists Bob Woodward and Carl Bernstein of the Washington Post played a key role in exposing the scandal, aided by a secret inside source nicknamed 'Deep Throat,' who was later revealed to be FBI Deputy Director Mark Felt.

## Significance
Watergate matters enormously in American history for several reasons. First, it showed that even the most powerful person in the country — the President — is not above the law. The fact that Nixon was forced to resign proved that the American system of checks and balances (the way different parts of government limit each other's power) could work even under extreme pressure. Second, it deeply damaged Americans' trust in their government, a feeling of suspicion that lasted for decades. Third, it led to new laws designed to make politics more honest, including stricter rules on campaign finance (how politicians raise and spend money). Finally, it demonstrated the vital role of a free press — the work of journalists Woodward and Bernstein showed that independent reporting can hold powerful people accountable and protect democracy.

## Key Facts
- On June 17, 1972, five burglars connected to President Nixon's re-election committee were arrested breaking into the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate complex in Washington, D.C.
- Nixon participated in a cover-up of White House involvement, secretly ordering the FBI to limit its investigation — a fact proven by secret tape recordings of his Oval Office conversations.
- Facing impeachment by the House of Representatives for obstruction of justice, abuse of power, and contempt of Congress, Nixon resigned on August 8, 1974, becoming the only U.S. president ever to resign from office.
- President Gerald Ford pardoned Nixon on September 8, 1974, sparing him from criminal prosecution.
- A total of 48 government officials were convicted of crimes related to the scandal, and the event led to lasting distrust of government and new laws on political ethics and campaign finance.
